Built From Nothing

I grew up in foster care in Greeley, Colorado. No safety net. No "Mom and Dad." I started working at 13—real work. Back-breaking work. The kind that leaves scars before you even know what health insurance is.

Ilsia came from a country the world calls "third-world," but together we built something no one expected—a family. For nearly 20 years, they said we wouldn't make it.

Now our son holds degrees in Criminal Justice and Computer Science. I'm a proud grandfather of two, with another on the way. My daughter is charging toward being a neuroscientist. Another serves in the U.S. Navy. The rest? Gifted, talented, smarter than most adults wish they were.

Every single one of my kids is what this country should be proud of. And yet, the system is trying to tear it all down.

The Wrongful Decision

Ilsia followed every step of the legal immigration process — and was still denied. Not for what she did, but for surviving.

She had an approved I-601A waiver — officially recognized by USCIS. She provided hospital records and court documents proving she was a victim of domestic violence.

But the Embassy called survival "fraud." They dismissed the facts. They ignored the law. They punished a woman for fleeing her abuser at 18 years old.

This isn't an immigration case — it's a miscarriage of justice. They're destroying an American family because they can.

Our Reality Now

We are stranded in a place where even brushing my teeth could mean hospital time. The water is contaminated—bottled water is a privilege, not a guarantee. Honduras had a homicide rate of ~34 per 100,000 in 2023—nearly six times the global average. Cartel violence, kidnappings, and disappearances are everyday threats, especially in areas like San Pedro Sula. Local authorities are overwhelmed and under-resourced, making safety and justice difficult to guarantee.

Meanwhile, our family home in Colorado is slipping away because of a wrongful immigration decision. This isn't exile—it's survival.

We are not "immigrants"—we are Americans, trapped outside our own home.

Every day in Central America feels like a countdown—not just to losing our house, but to losing each other. And while we fight to survive here, the bank back in Colorado is preparing to erase everything we built—because of a broken immigration system that refused to see us as human.

The Legal Injustice

Three False Claims That Changed Everything

INA §212(a)(9)(A)(ii)
⚠️ FALSE CLAIM #1
The embassy falsely claimed Ilsia had a prior deportation. In reality, there was no deportation order — only a voluntary return in 1998 after she fled domestic violence. An approved I-212 waiver made this ground no longer valid.
INA §212(a)(9)(B)(i)(II)
⚠️ FALSE CLAIM #2
This "unlawful presence" bar was already resolved. Ilsia received an approved I-601A waiver in February 2024. Citing it again at the visa interview was unjustified and ignored her lawful steps.
INA §212(a)(6)(C)(i)
⚠️ FALSE CLAIM #3
Ilsia used a different last name in 1998 while fleeing her abuser. At just 18 years old, terrified and vulnerable, she followed guidance from others to avoid being sent back to Nicaragua — where her life was in danger. No immigration benefit was gained, and case law confirms this is not fraud. But the embassy ignored this and misapplied the law.
